# Statement of Iranian Students
## Addressed to the Conscience of This Land and Its History

We, the students of universities across Iran, sign this statement.

---

## One: The Tallest Bridge in the Middle East and the Smallest Politicians in History

On the thirteenth of Farvardin, 1405 — April 2nd, 2026 — Bridge B1 in Karaj collapsed. The tallest bridge in the Middle East. 136 meters above the ground. Built by the hands of Iranian engineers, with the blood of Iranian workers, with the money of those same people who today are crushed beneath seventy-percent inflation.

That same day, a man sitting behind a desk thousands of kilometers away — a man whose country was built from the very beginning on the waves of migration of peoples like us, and a significant portion of whose doctors, engineers, and scientists are of Iranian origin — said out loud: "We will return Iran to the Stone Age."

Stop. And hear this clearly.

This insult was only possible for one fundamental reason. When your representatives, at every negotiating table from Vienna to Geneva, are people who know neither the language of diplomacy, nor the techniques of international bargaining, nor even how to write Persian as it should be written; when your strategists are those who spent lifetimes in study but have today been forced into retirement; when your international relations experts are unemployed because the rooms of decision have been handed over to inexperienced bravado — then even irresponsible men like Trump find the courage to tell a nation with three thousand years of civilization, of poetry and science and architecture and philosophy, of Hafez and Ibn Sina and Ferdowsi, that they will be sent back to the Stone Age.

This shame belongs to none of us. This shame belongs to the semi-literate politicians who for forty-eight years, under the claim of "representing Iran," have destroyed the image of this nation before the world.

And in these very days, this very apparatus — the one whose bridge collapsed, the one that could not prevent open insult — repeats the same words again: "This is a sensitive moment in history."

It said this in 2009. It said it in 2017. It said it in 2019. It said it in 2022. It said it in January 2026. And now, with war, with the sound of bombs, with skies that have become highways for foreign warplanes, it says it again.

We know what they will answer: "Who said those moments weren't historic? Iran stands against the infidel world, so every moment of it is historic."

We recognize this argument, because for centuries it has been the tool of every incompetent apparatus that cannot give a simple answer to a simple question: if every moment is sensitive, then why, after forty-eight years of "sensitive moments," are the people of Iran poorer, more isolated, and less hopeful? If all these moments were historic, where is the legacy of this history? Seventy-percent inflation? The flight of 165,000 specialists per year? A bridge that collapses?

"Sensitive historic moment" in the mouth of this apparatus means one thing and one thing only: be silent. be united. stand beneath our flag. ask no questions. and if you do ask, you are a spy, an agent of foreigners, a traitor.

**We say: No.**

History has shown that every incompetent apparatus that has no answer for its failures covers its weakness with the drum of "national unity" and charges its mistakes to the account of "foreign enemies." This time is the same — with the difference that this time, the back rooms no longer turn down the volume; because they have cut the internet so the sound will not be heard at all.

## Two: The Internet — A Crime That Must Be Answered For

The internet blackout was not a "mistake." It was not a "necessity." It was not a "security measure."

The internet blackout was an economic, social, and cultural crime, and this apparatus must answer for it before every Iranian citizen.

In the days when the skies of Iran were engulfed, millions of Iranians were wounded not by bombs but by this blackout. They justified it as a "security" measure. But we ask: whose security?

Online businesses that went bankrupt overnight — their security? Freelancers who had multi-month foreign currency contracts that were annulled overnight — their security? The student who had to submit a thesis — their security? The doctor who needed to read an international journal — their security? The mother who wanted to know which city her child was alive in — her security?

We know what they will say: "It was an exceptional situation. To prevent espionage. And we even thought of the people and built domestic infrastructure."

Let us examine this.

"Preventing espionage" — meaning a country that claims regional power, to prevent intelligence infiltration, knows no other way than to cut the internet of more than eighty-five million of its own citizens? A country that claims advancement in science and technology is so incapable in cybersecurity that its only solution is complete shutdown? This is not a sign of strength. It is a sign of technical incompetence, and a hand reaching for the crudest tool available.

And "domestic infrastructure" — the network you built to replace the free internet — is the very tool with which you make censorship more complete, not the welfare of the people. A network in which you decide what people see, what they read, who they speak with — this is not "thinking of the people." This is control. This is a digital prison with the name "National Internet."

No. This is the security of an apparatus that fears the truth. An apparatus that knows if images of what it does are published, even those who believe in it with their whole being — neighbors, teachers, relatives — will be repulsed.

And the most bitter layer of this crime is that at the very moment millions of Iranians were cut off from the internet, officials of this same apparatus used their own high-speed dedicated internet freely. For those who made the decision, nothing was cut.

And more bitter still: part of the infrastructure of this very censorship is built and maintained by those who benefited from these same universities, these same academic associations, this same national budget. Knowledge that should have created freedom became chains. And those who forged those chains were never in them.

Every hour of the blackout, every cancelled contract, every lost opportunity, every child whose education fell behind, every patient whose file arrived late, must be charged to the account of those who made this decision — in every court, in every international legal institution, in every media outlet that hears our voice.

## Three: The Legacy of Forty-Eight Years — What the Numbers Say, and What Their Pain Cannot Measure

This apparatus has for forty-eight years promoted "resistance economics." Let us see what this legacy is — not with slogans, not with the lofty rhetoric of revolution, but with those stubborn numbers that no propaganda can hide.

Iran's GDP per capita in 2024 is approximately $5,190. Turkey: thirteen thousand. Saudi Arabia: thirty thousand. The UAE: fifty thousand. South Korea, which was at the same level as Iran in the 1960s: thirty-three thousand. Iran, with the world's largest hydrocarbon reserves, with an educated population, with a strategically positioned geography — yet with one-fifth the per capita income of Turkey.

Inflation: seventy percent. The dollar, which stood at a few thousand tomans a decade ago, has today reached 160,000 tomans. Write that number out: 160,000. This means the lifetime savings of an Iranian family, in the span of a few years, have been reduced to a fraction of their original value. The rial has lost seventy-five percent of its value between 2023 and 2026. This is no longer inflation; this is the slow-motion collapse of a nation's assets.

Youth unemployment: twenty-two percent, seven points above the global average. Women's participation in the labor market: only thirteen percent, against a global average of forty-nine percent. Brain drain: 165,000 specialists — doctors, engineers, researchers — leave this land every year. The human cost of this exodus: $150 to $170 billion per year.

The wage of an Iranian worker today, with all their skill and effort, is less than one hundred dollars a month. One hundred dollars. That same worker, with those same calloused hands, in any neighboring country, would earn several times that. And the number "one hundred dollars" is not merely a statistic; behind it is a breakfast eaten without eggs, a child's school clothes that bring shame, a dream that a father has swallowed so his son won't even ask about it.

But these cold numbers still do not tell the full pain. We must speak of something that is never written in official reports: of what could have been.

Iran has had an automotive industry since the early 1950s. Over seventy years of history. Engineering human capital. A large domestic market. Iran could have been one of the world's automotive powerhouses today — not a factory reproducing the forty-year-old design of a European company and calling it a "national product." South Korean brothers, in the same period, went from nothing to Hyundai and Kia. We, with fifty more years of history, still walk the same path of repeating yesterday's models — because every time an engineer had a vision, instead of investment in their thinking, they were punished for their independence of thought.

Iran holds one of the world's greatest reserves of oil and gas. Yet today we are a country struggling with electricity shortages. Household power is cut. Factories shut down. Winter gas does not reach distant cities. An energy shortage in Iran should be a bitter joke — an impossible paradox — yet it is the daily reality of millions. The money from this energy has gone somewhere; but not to a grid that delivers it into the hands of people. It has gone to budgets that the people never see an accounting of.

Iran, with its geographic position, its unparalleled historical attractions, its diverse climate, its seas to the north and south, its star-filled deserts and northern forests, could have been one of the world's great tourism destinations. Tourism revenue could have been tens of billions of dollars per year — jobs not dependent on oil exports, not vulnerable to sanctions, wealth that reaches directly into the hands of ordinary people. Instead, foreign travelers are frightened by visa complexities, diplomatic isolation, and legal uncertainty, and this beautiful land remains empty.

And in place of all of this — in place of a global automotive industry, a modern energy sector, a thriving tourism economy, a modern aviation industry that people would step off of with pride — what do we have? Missiles. Drones. And the slogan of "the threshold of the collapse of the global order." Those who made this choice held hostage not only an economic future, but the sense of dignity of an entire nation.

This gap — between what we could have been and what we are — cannot be fully expressed in any number. But it is felt in the pain of the engineer who wakes each morning knowing that his mind, his skill, the years of his education, will never build here what they could have built. In the pain of the worker who, with one hundred dollars a month, in a country of vast underground wealth, calculates every month whether he can pay rent and also buy food. In the pain of the student who knows that the best years of her education were spent in a country that filters international scientific resources and knows scientific advancement only in advertisements.

This is the legacy. Not missiles. Not drones. Not the claim of "the threshold of the collapse of the American order." The legacy is that a young Iranian today lives on one-fifth the per capita income of their Turkish peer. The legacy is that ten classmates from Tehran University now work in Germany and Canada and Australia. The legacy is that "scientific advancement" is advertised while most international academic platforms are blocked.

And these numbers belonged to the "best-case scenario before the war." Now, with war, with destroyed infrastructure, with deeper isolation, all of these numbers have worsened. Much worse.

This is not "progress." This is the historical record of incompetence — incompetence draped in slogans.

## Four: Destroyed Infrastructure, Flourishing Political Market

The skies of Iran have become a highway for foreign warplanes. Read that sentence again.

The skies of a land whose people only want to work, to study, to breathe — these skies are today a battlefield for powers that care nothing for the people of Iran. Not America and Israel, who came with sanctions and bombs. And not the apparatus that, with its aggressive decisions, its support for armed groups in four countries, its vicious cycle of "provocation-sanction-justification," fanned these flames.

Every piece of infrastructure targeted in Iran today, every bridge that collapses, every power plant that burns, every network that goes down — was built by the hands of Iranian engineers. With the money of the Iranian people. With the sweat of Iranian workers. But the apparatus that for years pushed an aggressive regional policy against all expert warnings now reaps political benefit from the destruction of this infrastructure. We recognize this trap: first they prepare the ground for attack, then with the image of the attack, they purchase their domestic justification for survival.

A foreigner struck the Karaj Bridge — but what placed the Karaj Bridge in the crosshairs was decided here. And this distinction — between "foreign enemy" and "domestic responsibility" — is one this apparatus has never accepted.

They sold the Ramadan War to the people of Iran as a "historic achievement." But ask the family in Minab that places bread and cheese on the grave of their Fatemeh — what achievement? Ask the office worker who woke to find his internet cut, his business shuttered — what achievement? Ask the student whose months-long foreign currency freelance contract was annulled by the internet blackout — what achievement?

The reality is this: the rising price of energy carriers across the world is not an achievement for the people of Iran. That money does not go into the pockets of the people. This "trump card" is held by an apparatus that uses it to purchase its own survival, not the welfare of its people. Helping raise the price of gasoline in Europe while the people of Iran are crushed beneath seventy-percent inflation is not a victory. It is the continuation of the same eternal pattern: the people pay the cost, the apparatus takes the profit.

China is also sanctioned, yet it is the world's second-largest economy. India also faced sanctions, but found a diplomatic path. Iran could have too. But "resistance" for this apparatus is a tool of power, not a tool of progress. They continue the war because their survival depends on it — not the survival of Iran.

## Five: The Minority That Did Not Build Iran But Fed From It

We must speak plainly about an unspoken reality.

In this land there exists a small minority that has built no bridge, established no factory, trained no student, conducted no research, opened no bakery, healed no patient on a bed. Yet for forty-eight years it has made decisions about all of these things — and called anyone with the courage to ask questions a "spy," an "agent of foreigners," a "counter-revolutionary."

This is the same minority that made the ordinary lives of people hell for decades. The ones who deemed going to a café a crime. The ones who deemed listening to music a crime. The ones who required that the manner of dress, manner of speech, manner of laughter all conform to their measure. The ones who sent the morality police to erase even the simplest signs of ordinary life from the streets — so that people would forget that a normal life, in all its ordinariness, is the natural right of every human being. The ones who shut down music concerts, closed restaurants for "mixed" spaces, and called the glance between a boy and a girl in a park "corruption."

And now, this same minority posts photographs of those very cafés on social media to prove "look how good Iran is." The same restaurants that they once shuttered with their own hands are today offered as evidence of "progress." The same streets their morality police once patrolled are today framed as "peace" in their promotional cameras.

This is a lie. And everyone knows it is a lie.

Iran was built by its people. Built by its workers. Built by its engineers. Built by its teachers. Built by its doctors. Built by its farmers. Built by the small businesses that opened their doors every morning. Those who built were these ordinary people — the same ones this radical minority tormented for years, silenced, and whose achievements it now confiscates in its own name.

Those who today most loudly advertise "war" do not have their children in the front lines. Those who most readily sell "resistance" sit behind their own high-speed dedicated internet. Those who invite others to "silence in the sensitive moment" have never themselves paid the price of these "sensitive moments" — not with the blood of their children, not with inflation in their pockets, not with the loss of their jobs.

One who has never built this land has no right to set its price. And one who has never paid the cost of war has no right to declare it.

## Six: Dividing Iranians — The Last Weapon of a Besieged Apparatus

When people took to the streets in 2009, they said "foreign agents." When they came in 2017, they said "rioters." When they came in 2019, they said "counter-revolutionaries." When they came in 2022, they said "foreign proxies." When they came in January 2026, they said "the enemy's parade."

But we know who these people are. Because they are us. They are students at Sharif University. Students at the University of Tehran. Students at Amirkabir, Iran University of Science and Technology, Isfahan, Tabriz, Mashhad, Shiraz, Ahvaz. Factory workers. Schoolteachers. Bazaar merchants. Farmers from Faryab. Mothers standing in bank queues.

These people are not Mossad spies. They are not CIA agents. These are the people who for forty-eight years heard "be patient, things will improve" and were patient. Who for forty-eight years heard "stay and build" and stayed and built. Who for forty-eight years paid the price and received in return only inflation and unemployment and censorship.

And now, this apparatus has constructed another division: twenty-two million Iranians are outside the borders. More than eighty-five million are inside. They want to make two "Irans" of these two — those who "believe" and those who "are traitors," those who are "insiders" and those who are "dependent." This division has one purpose and one purpose only: to prevent the moment when these two large populations speak to one another, plan together, imagine a better Iran together.

But remember one thing: anyone who says this statement is a "reformist project," anyone who says "this is the voice of monarchists," anyone who wants to dismiss the content of these demands by attaching a label — has a simple question before them: did the Karaj Bridge really collapse? Is the worker's wage really under one hundred dollars? Is the student really in prison? If the answer to these is "yes," then the label on the speaker has nothing to do with the truth of what is spoken. We do not represent any political discourse. We represent these realities.

This is the cheap game of every weak apparatus that no longer has any real victory to offer. When you have no economy, no diplomacy, no welfare, no freedom, the only thing left is the production of enemies. Internal enemies. Foreign enemies. Cultural enemies. Political enemies. Internet enemies.

But we know that the Sharif student whose dormitory door was pounded, and the Iranian engineer in Berlin who wept for them, are both Iranian. Both concerned for this same soil. This apparatus cannot sever that bond — however much it cuts the internet, however many walls it builds, however many "insiders" and "outsiders" it carves out.

We, the free people of Iran, reject this division. With every hand, with every voice, with every tool we have.

## Seven: Forty-Eight Years, and a Bitter Resemblance That Can No Longer Be Hidden

We are a generation born in the shadow of sanctions. We grew up in bread lines. We studied with a rial that shrank every year. And throughout all these years we were told: the "foreign enemy" is to blame. "Global imperialism" is to blame. "The Zionist conspiracy" is to blame. "Foreign agents" are to blame.

But we grew up. We learned. We read the data. We saw history with our own eyes — not from approved textbooks. And we arrived at a simple conclusion that is painful to say, but whose silence is more painful still:

An apparatus that for forty-eight years has claimed to fight "tyranny" has, over these forty-eight years, in many respects, fallen no shorter than the very "tyranny" it claimed to fight — if not surpassed it.

We say this not out of enmity, but out of seeing reality:

Those who took to the streets in 2009 were imprisoned. Those who came in 2017 were killed. Those who came in 2019 were shot. Those who came in 2022 were mutilated. Those who came in January 2026 remain in cold storage to this day.

But January 2026 must not be equated with prior years. January 2026 was a new chapter of terror — not only in scale, but in depth and in openness. The scenes that were witnessed, the accounts that emerged, the brazenness with which blood was shed — all of it was so unguarded that it shattered even the silence of thousands who for years had called every protest "the foreign hand." Those who were in the streets of January 2026 know. And those who were not there but heard the news could not sleep until morning. This cannot be finished with the single sentence "they remain in cold storage." This must be said — loudly, clearly, without cover.

These were not Mossad spies. They were not CIA agents. They were our teachers. Our neighbors. Our parents. Ourselves.

An apparatus that treats its people this way has proven in practice that "independence," "justice," and "the people" in its mouth are nothing more than instruments of legitimation. And we, the free people of Iran, declare that we will no longer endure it. Not from this apparatus. Not from any other apparatus. Not from any power that purchases its survival at our expense — from wherever it has come, by whatever name it has come.

## Eight: Where We Stand

Parnian Khodabakhshi, a twenty-year-old student who enrolled in 2023, is in security detention facilities — without a warrant, without a lawyer. Reza Dalman, a graduate student in computer engineering, was taken from his home on the Thursday night before the new year. Meanwhile, the apparatus that claims to protect national security leaves infiltrated spies within its own core untouched, and arrests students.

This apparatus is not powerful. It is terrified. Terrified of a generation that no longer falls for the "historic moment." Terrified of a young person who, when told "China is also sanctioned but...," already knows the answer. Terrified of a student who knows the numbers, knows the history, and does not stay silent.

## Nine: What We Demand — Not a Request, Not a Plea

The time for waiting is over. The time for "be patient, things will improve" has passed — because we were patient for forty-eight years and "things" only grew heavier. The time for "stay and build" also has meaning only when they let us stay and build — but everyone who had the courage to build has either left the country or sits in prison.

We do not ask of this apparatus. **We demand.** With the voice of citizens who built this land with their own hands.

**One — End the war.** Every day this war continues, a child in Iran loses their future. Every day this war continues, a mother is bereaved, a worker goes bankrupt, a student drops out. "Victory" in a war where the country's skies are highways for foreign aircraft and the price of bread rises every week is not victory — it is the confiscation of the people's misery in the name of "national pride."

**Two — Open the internet.** Millions of businesses, millions of job opportunities, millions of students, millions of human connections — all sacrificed in the name of "security." A country that advertises "scientific advancement" but filters international academic resources is not "resisting," not "progressing" — it is only purchasing isolation.

**Three — Free the students.** Parnian Khodabakhshi, Reza Dalman, and every student and protester in detention for expressing dissent must be released immediately. An apparatus that imprisons its country's future-builders to maintain power signs the certificate of its own incompetence with its own hand.

**Four — Return policy to the hands of specialists.** The cycle of "regional aggression, sanctions, justification" must stop. There is no crisis without a solution, but the solutions are in the hands of specialists who have been forced into retirement for years.

And if you have the capacity for none of this, **open the way.**

## Ten: A Call to the Free People of Iran, Wherever You Are

We — students, workers, teachers, doctors, engineers, artists, farmers, those in Tehran, those in Zahedan, those in Berlin, those in Toronto — all together and with one voice declare:

**Take your lives back.** The café that today the government photographs to say "see how peaceful Iran is" — that was the same café they once shuttered for "corruption." The restaurant that today is offered as evidence of "welfare" was the same one sealed for "mixed spaces." The street that today appears peaceful in their promotional cameras was the same street the morality police patrolled, seeking the simplest signs of ordinary life to call a crime. The people of Iran did not create this peace — the people of Iran lived despite all of that pressure. And now do not allow those who once treated normal life as a crime to purchase legitimacy from the image of that life.

**Document everything.** Every internet blackout, every warrantless arrest, every piece of destroyed infrastructure, every lost economic opportunity — record it. History cannot be erased by cutting the internet. Every international institution, every human rights tribunal, every competent organization must know that we will come to them.

**In every gathering, in every meeting, speak the truth.** At every family dinner, every gathering of friends, every small conversation, do not allow radical propaganda to silence the reasonable voice of the majority. Do not call those who want peace "agents." Recognize those who advertise war without paying its price. Refute lies one by one, honestly, without fear. No lie should go unanswered — not at home, not at work, not at university, not on social media.

**Be each other's voice.** Every free Iranian whose voice carries — whether in media, in social networks, in academic settings, in international institutions — has a duty to show the world the true image of this nation, against the distorted image a radical and incompetent minority has constructed. The real Iran is the poetry of Hafez. The real Iran is the science of Ibn Sina. The real Iran is the student at Sharif who studies even from behind bars. Not missiles. Not proxies. Not war.

**Move past division.** Every effort to pit Iranians inside and outside the borders against each other, every narrative trying to create "insiders" and "outsiders," we recognize it and we reject it. We are one nation. With one history. With one language. With one shared dream: an Iran in which one can breathe. This dream is neither monarchist nor reformist, neither left nor right. It is the dream of every human being who breathes this soil and loves it.

The protesters of 2009, 2017, 2019, 2022, and January 2026 were these same people. These same students. These same workers, teachers, nurses, farmers, drivers. They were not "foreign agents." And these people — we — remain. If this apparatus does not itself begin fundamental change, history has shown that the people of Iran have never made peace with any lock forever.

## Final: This Land Is Ours — It Belongs to No Discourse But Freedom

In the midst of all this dust, in days when bridges collapse, internet is cut, foreign warplanes cleave our skies, and irresponsible men from thousands of kilometers away threaten to return a nation with three thousand years of civilization "to the Stone Age," one thing has remained constant:

The majority of the people of this land want a prosperous Iran. Neither the discourse of "resistance" that has brought only poverty for forty-eight years, nor the discourse of "resurrection" from beyond the borders that has never once set foot on this soil, listens to this simple desire.

People who want to work. To study. To breathe. To have access to the internet. To build their own tomorrow. Minds that leave do not return. A girl who is arrested does not study. A child whose school is bombed does not grow up. These costs are paid by all of our futures — including those who today profit from this rupture.

We are here. We exist. We have pens. We have voices. We have data. We have history. We have the truth.

No internet blackout has been able to take this from us. No arrest has been able to take this from us. No "historic moment" — which in the mouth of this apparatus means "be silent" — has been able to take this from us.

**This land belongs to no discourse. It belongs to everyone who breathes in it, toils in it, and cares for it.**

We demand of this apparatus — not with pleading, not with request, but with the full-standing demand of citizens who built this land:

End the war. Open the internet. Free the students. Return policy to the hands of specialists.
*"And if you have the capacity for none of this, open the way."*

Because the history of this land has shown that the people of Iran have never remained before any lock forever.

History is being written. This time, the pen is in our hands.
